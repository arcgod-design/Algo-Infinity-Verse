Work-Stealing Scheduler

Own queue for speed. Steal from others for balance.

Every worker owns a private double-ended queue. It pushes and pops its OWN new work from the front — LIFO, cache-friendly. When idle, it steals from the BACK of someone else's queue — FIFO, taking the oldest, most parallelizable work. This nearly eliminates lock contention compared to a single shared queue.

🧵 Why Work-Stealing Wins

📥

LIFO for Cache Locality

A worker pops its OWN most-recently-pushed task first. That task's data was just touched — still hot in L1/L2 cache. This is why work-stealing outperforms naive round-robin scheduling on real hardware, not just in Big-O terms.

🔓

FIFO Stealing Minimizes Collisions

Thieves steal from the opposite end (back) of a victim's deque — the oldest, coarsest-grained task. This means the owner (popping from front) and thieves (popping from back) almost never touch the same end at once, minimizing lock contention to near zero.

⚖️

Provably Balanced

Work-stealing schedulers achieve expected O(T∞ + P) execution time for T∞ critical-path length and P processors — a theoretical guarantee proven by Blumofe and Leiserson in 1994, still the basis of Cilk, Go, and Java's ForkJoinPool today.

🏃

Go's GMP Model

Go's runtime scheduler (Goroutines, Machine threads, Processors) uses exactly this pattern — each P has a local run queue; idle Ps steal from busy ones. This is why goroutine-heavy Go programs scale near-linearly across cores without manual tuning.
